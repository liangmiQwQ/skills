# User Research Methods

**Purpose:** Complete guide to understanding users through systematic research methods — from discovery to validation.

**Principle:** User research is not an optional add-on. It's the foundation of user-centered design. Without research, you're designing for assumptions, not people.

---

## 1. Why User Research Matters

### The Business Case

**Principle:** Research reduces risk and increases ROI.**

**Impact:**
- **Cost savings:** Fixing problems after launch costs 100x more than during design
- **Competitive advantage:** Understanding user needs creates differentiated experiences
- **Reduced abandonment:** Products based on research have 2-3x higher retention

**The reality:**
Even the best UX designers cannot design optimal experiences without observing real users. The number of variables in modern interfaces and human behavior is too large to rely on intuition alone.

### When to Do Research

**Research happens throughout the design process:**

1. **Discover (Before Design):** Understand user needs and context
2. **Explore (During Design):** Validate design decisions and directions
3. **Test (Before Launch):** Ensure usability and effectiveness
4. **Listen (After Launch):** Monitor performance and uncover new opportunities

## 2. Types of User Research

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative

**Qualitative Research:**
- **Purpose:** Understand "why" users behave the way they do
- **Methods:** Interviews, usability testing, field studies, diary studies
- **Output:** Insights, patterns, quotes, anecdotes
- **Sample Size:** Small (5-10 participants per user group)
- **Best for:** Discovering problems, understanding motivations, exploring opportunities

**Quantitative Research:**
- **Purpose:** Measure "what" users do at scale
- **Methods:** Surveys, analytics, A/B testing, heat maps
- **Output:** Metrics, percentages, statistical significance
- **Sample Size:** Large (100+ participants)
- **Best for:** Validating assumptions, benchmarking, tracking trends

### Attitudinal vs. Behavioral

**Attitudinal Research:**
- **What it is:** Listening to what users say
- **Methods:** Interviews, surveys, focus groups
- **Use when:** You want to understand opinions, preferences, or self-reported behavior
- **Risk:** What people say differs from what they do

**Behavioral Research:**
- **What it is:** Watching what users actually do
- **Methods:** Usability testing, field studies, analytics
- **Use when:** You want to understand real behavior and interactions
- **Advantage:** Actions speak louder than words

**Best practice:** Combine both approaches for the sharpest insights.

## 3. Core Research Methods

### Usability Testing

**Principle:** Observe real users performing real tasks with your design.**

**What it is:**
A facilitator guides a participant through tasks using an interface while observing behavior and listening to feedback.

**Key Elements:**
- **Facilitator:** Guides the test, asks follow-up questions, ensures data quality
- **Tasks:** Realistic activities users would perform in real life
- **Participant:** Should represent your target user group

**Sample Size:**
- **Qualitative:** 5 participants per user group uncovers 80% of problems
- **Quantitative:** 20+ participants for statistically significant metrics

**Types of Usability Testing:**

1. **In-Person:**
   - Facilitator and participant in same room
   - Rich observational data (body language, facial expressions)
   - Higher cost and logistics

2. **Remote Moderated:**
   - Facilitator and participant in different locations
   - Screen sharing (Skype, Zoom, GoToMeeting)
   - Lower cost, geographic flexibility

3. **Remote Unmoderated:**
   - Participant completes tasks alone using online platform
   - Automated task delivery and recording
   - Scalable but limited interaction

**Best Practices:**
- Use the "think-aloud" method: ask participants to narrate their thoughts
- Write task instructions carefully — avoid leading language
- Test your tasks first (pilot test) to catch issues
- Record sessions for analysis (with consent)

### Interviews

**Principle:** Direct conversations uncover deep insights about user needs, motivations, and contexts.**

**Types:**

1. **Contextual Inquiry:**
   - Interview users in their own environment
   - Observe them performing tasks in context
   - Reveals environmental and workflow factors

2. **Semi-Structured Interviews:**
   - Open-ended questions with a flexible guide
   - Allows exploration of unexpected topics
   - Balances consistency with depth

3. **Stakeholder Interviews:**
   - Interview business stakeholders about goals and constraints
   - Understand organizational context and requirements
   - Align research with business objectives

**Best Practices:**
- Ask "why" repeatedly to dig deeper (laddering technique)
- Avoid leading questions that suggest answers
- Listen more than you speak (80/20 rule)
- Record and transcribe for analysis

### Surveys

**Principle:** Surveys gather data at scale from many users quickly.**

**When to use:**
- Validate findings from qualitative research
- Gather demographic information
- Track satisfaction over time
- Collect self-reported behaviors and preferences

**Best Practices:**
- Keep it short (5-10 questions max)
- Use simple, unambiguous language
- Mix question types (multiple choice, rating scales, open-ended)
- Test your survey first (pilot with 5-10 people)
- Offer incentives to increase response rates

**Question types:**
- **Multiple choice:** Easy to analyze, limited depth
- **Rating scales:** Quantifiable attitudes (Likert scales)
- **Open-ended:** Rich insights, harder to analyze
- **Ranking:** Understand priorities and preferences

### Field Studies

**Principle:** Observe users in their natural environment to understand context and behavior.**

**What it reveals:**
- Environmental factors affecting usage
- Workflow and process context
- Social and cultural influences
- Pain points not visible in lab settings

**Types:**
1. **Ethnographic Studies:** Immersive observation over extended periods
2. **Contextual Inquiry:** Interview + observation during task performance
3. **Shadowing:** Following users through their day

**Best Practices:**
- Plan observation periods (2-4 hours typical)
- Take detailed notes and photos (with permission)
- Look for workarounds and hacks — they reveal unmet needs
- Stay neutral, don't intervene unless necessary

### Diary Studies

**Principle:** Users record their experiences over time, revealing patterns and context.**

**What it captures:**
- Longitudinal behavior and attitudes
- Context of use (when, where, why)
- Emotional journey over time
- In-the-moment reflections

**Implementation:**
- Participants log activities in a diary (digital or physical)
- Prompts ask about specific behaviors, contexts, or feelings
- Duration: 1 week to 1 month typical
- Daily check-ins maintain engagement

**Best Practices:**
- Keep diary entries short (2-3 minutes)
- Use mobile-friendly digital diaries
- Send daily reminders to participants
- Offer incentives for completion
- Analyze for patterns over time

### Card Sorting

**Principle:** Users organize information into categories, revealing mental models.**

**When to use:**
- Design information architecture
- Organize navigation structures
- Plan content hierarchies
- Understand user categorization logic

**Types:**
1. **Open Card Sort:** Users create their own categories
2. **Closed Card Sort:** Users sort into predefined categories
3. **Hybrid Card Sort:** Mixed approach with some predefined categories

**Best Practices:**
- Use 30-60 cards maximum
- Label cards clearly with user-facing terms
- Test your cards first (are they understandable?)
- Analyze for patterns, not consensus
- Consider remote tools for scalability

## 4. Research Process

### Step 1: Define Research Questions

**Start with clear objectives.**

Good research questions:
- Specific and focused
- Answerable with chosen methods
- Aligned with business goals
- Grounded in assumptions to test

**Examples:**
- "Why do users abandon checkout at the payment step?"
- "How do users currently track their expenses?"
- "What features do users expect in a mobile banking app?"

### Step 2: Choose Methods

**Match methods to questions:**

| Research Goal | Best Methods |
|---------------|--------------|
| Understand user needs | Interviews, field studies, diary studies |
| Test usability | Usability testing (moderated) |
| Validate at scale | Surveys, analytics, A/B testing |
| Explore information needs | Card sorting, tree testing |
| Observe natural behavior | Field studies, diary studies |

**Consider constraints:**
- Time available
- Budget
- Access to participants
- Tools and expertise

### Step 3: Recruit Participants

**Principle:** Recruit users who represent your target audience.**

**Recruitment channels:**
- **User database:** Existing customers or users
- **Recruitment agencies:** Professional services (UserResearch.com, User Interviews)
- **Social media:** Targeted ads and posts
- **Referrals:** Current participants refer others
- **Intercept recruiting:** Approaching users in context

**Screening criteria:**
- Demographics (age, location, role)
- Experience level (novice vs. expert)
- Usage patterns (frequency, features used)
- Technical setup (device, browser, internet)

**Incentives:**
- Monetary compensation ($50-150 per session typical)
- Gift cards or product discounts
- Early access to features
- Donation to charity

**Sample size guidance:**
- **Qualitative:** 5-10 participants per user group
- **Quantitative:** 100+ for statistical significance
- **Card sorting:** 15-30 participants
- **Surveys:** 200+ for reliable data

### Step 4: Prepare Research Materials

**Essential materials:**

1. **Research Plan:**
   - Objectives and questions
   - Methods and timeline
   - Participant criteria
   - Discussion guide or tasks

2. **Discussion Guide (for interviews):**
   - Introduction and consent
   - Warm-up questions
   - Core questions aligned with objectives
   - Closing and thank you

3. **Task Scenarios (for usability testing):**
   - Realistic activities
   - Clear starting point
   - Success criteria
   - Avoid leading language

4. **Consent Form:**
   - Purpose of research
   - What will be recorded
   - How data will be used
   - Right to withdraw

### Step 5: Conduct Research

**Best practices during sessions:**

- **Build rapport:** Start with casual conversation
- **Stay neutral:** Don't influence responses
- **Probe deeper:** Ask "why" and "tell me more"
- **Record everything:** Audio, video, notes (with consent)
- **Be flexible:** Follow interesting threads
- **Watch the clock:** Respect participant time

**Common pitfalls:**
- Leading questions ("Don't you think...?")
- Interrupting participants
- Jumping to solutions
- Confirming bias (seeking only agreement)
- Ignoring body language

### Step 6: Analyze Data

**Qualitative Analysis:**

1. **Affinity Diagramming:**
   - Group observations and quotes by theme
   - Identify patterns across participants
   - Surface insights and opportunities

2. **Thematic Analysis:**
   - Code data for recurring themes
   - Map relationships between themes
   - Illustrate with quotes and examples

**Quantitative Analysis:**

1. **Descriptive Statistics:**
   - Averages, percentages, distributions
   - Task completion rates, time on task
   - Satisfaction scores (SUS, NPS)

2. **Inferential Statistics:**
   - Compare groups (A/B testing)
   - Test significance (p-values)
   - Correlations and relationships

**Best practices:**
- Start analysis immediately after sessions
- Use both top-down (research questions) and bottom-up (emerging themes) approaches
- Triangulate findings (confirm across methods)
- Distinguish between what users say vs. do

### Step 7: Report Findings

**Principle:** Translate research into actionable insights.**

**Report structure:**

1. **Executive Summary:**
   - Key findings and recommendations
   - Business impact
   - 1-page summary for stakeholders

2. **Background:**
   - Research questions and objectives
   - Methods used
   - Participants and context

3. **Findings:**
   - Organized by theme or research question
   - Support with data (quotes, metrics, examples)
   - Distinguish between critical and nice-to-have

4. **Recommendations:**
   - Specific, actionable design changes
   - Prioritized by impact and effort
   - Aligned with business goals

5. **Appendices:**
   - Detailed methodology
   - Full transcripts (if needed)
   - Screening criteria and materials

**Presentation tips:**
- Start with key insights, not methodology
- Use video clips and quotes to bring findings to life
- Include stakeholders in the analysis process for buy-in
- Provide clear next steps

## 5. Research Timing

### Discovery Phase (Before Design)

**Goal:** Understand user needs, context, and pain points.

**Methods:**
- Stakeholder interviews
- User interviews
- Field studies
- Competitive analysis
- Analytics review

**Output:**
- User personas
- User needs and pain points
- Opportunity areas
- Design requirements

### Exploration Phase (During Design)

**Goal:** Validate design directions and explore solutions.

**Methods:**
- Card sorting
- Tree testing
- Concept testing
- Prototype testing
- A/B testing

**Output:**
- Validated design directions
- Information architecture
- Refined prototypes
- Prioritized features

### Validation Phase (Before Launch)

**Goal:** Ensure usability and effectiveness.

**Methods:**
- Usability testing (moderated)
- Usability testing (unmoderated)
- Accessibility testing
- Surveys (SUS, NPS)

**Output:**
- Usability benchmarks
- List of issues to fix
- Confidence in launch readiness
- Satisfaction metrics

### Listening Phase (After Launch)

**Goal:** Monitor performance and uncover opportunities.

**Methods:**
- Analytics review
- Surveys
- Feedback analysis
- Support ticket analysis
- A/B testing

**Output:**
- Performance dashboards
- Improvement backlog
- Optimization priorities
- Future research needs

## 6. Research Ethics

### Informed Consent

**Principle:** Participants must understand and agree to the research.**

**Consent form should include:**
- Purpose of research
- What will be recorded
- How data will be used and stored
- Who will see the data
- Right to withdraw without penalty
- Contact information for questions

### Privacy and Confidentiality

**Best practices:**
- Anonymize data in reports (use pseudonyms)
- Store data securely (encrypted, access-controlled)
- Retain data only as long as needed
- Don't share identifiable information without consent
- Follow GDPR and other regulations

### Avoiding Harm

**Ensure research:**
- Does not cause stress or discomfort
- Respects participant time
- Does not exploit vulnerable populations
- Provides fair compensation
- Minimizes risk

### Bias Awareness

**Common biases:**
- **Confirmation bias:** Seeking only data that supports assumptions
- **Leading questions:** Influencing responses through phrasing
- **Social desirability:** Participants saying what they think you want to hear
- **Recency bias:** Overweighting recent data
- **Hawthorne effect:** Participants changing behavior because they're observed

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Use neutral language in questions
- Triangulate findings across methods
- Include diverse perspectives in analysis
- Acknowledge limitations in reporting

## 7. Common Research Mistakes

### 1. Researching Too Late

**Problem:** Starting research after design decisions are made.

**Solution:** Research early and often throughout the process.

### 2. Wrong Participants

**Problem:** Testing with non-representative users (colleagues, friends).

**Solution:** Recruit participants who match your target user profile.

### 3. Leading Questions

**Problem:** Questions that suggest desired answers.

**Solution:** Use neutral, open-ended questions. Test your discussion guide first.

### 4. Too Many Research Questions

**Problem:** Trying to answer everything in one study.

**Solution:** Focus on 3-5 key questions per study. Do multiple studies if needed.

### 5. Ignoring Context

**Problem:** Researching in lab settings that don't reflect real use.

**Solution:** Combine lab testing with field studies and diary studies.

### 6. Analysis Paralysis

**Problem:** Collecting data but not analyzing or acting on it.

**Solution:** Start analysis immediately after sessions. Report quickly.

### 7. Research Without Action

**Problem:** Findings sit in reports but don't influence design.

**Solution:** Involve stakeholders in research. Present actionable recommendations.
